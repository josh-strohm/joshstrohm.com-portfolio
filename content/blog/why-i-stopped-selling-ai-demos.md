A demo can look like magic in a thirty-minute call. A production system has to work when nobody is watching — at 6 AM on a Sunday, when a lead comes in, when a follow-up was supposed to go out three days ago.

That gap is where most AI projects die. I stopped selling the gap.

## What a demo actually proves

A demo proves someone can wire up an API and write a convincing script. It does **not** prove:

- The workflow survives a weird edge case at 11 PM
- Your team will actually use the output
- The system recovers when a third-party tool changes its API
- Anyone will maintain it after the consultant leaves

*Impressive in a meeting* and *useful in operations* are different products.

## What changed in how I sell

I stopped leading with prototypes and started leading with workflows that already run for real businesses. The conversation shifts immediately:

1. **Before:** "Imagine if every inbound lead got a reply in under two minutes."
2. **After:** "Here's what happened last week — 47 leads, 41 booked, zero manual touches."

Instead of "imagine if," it becomes "here's what happened." Clients don't have to trust a vision. They can evaluate a track record.

> If I wouldn't run it on my own business, I won't ship it to yours.

## The standard I use now

Every build has to pass a simple test: **would I run this myself?**

Hermes, my own AI employee, handles my back office — scheduling, follow-ups, intake, the work that used to eat my afternoons. If I wouldn't trust a system with my own operations, I won't ship it to a client.

That means:

- Real error handling, not happy-path-only flows
- Logging you can actually read when something breaks
- A human handoff path that doesn't require a PhD to trigger
- Documentation written for the person who inherits it in six months

---

## What this means if you're hiring someone

Ask three questions before you sign anything:

1. Can they show you a system running in production — not a sandbox?
2. Who maintains it when the builder is gone?
3. What happens when the AI is wrong?

If the answers are vague, you're buying a demo. Demos are easy. Systems are the work.

That's the only thing worth selling — and the only thing worth buying.
